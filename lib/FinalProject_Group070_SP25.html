Abstract¶

Smartphones consume many young adults' everyday lives, serving more than just a tool for communication, but as entertainment, distraction, and emotional stimulation. Behaviors like “doom scrolling” and a heavy reliance on phones have contributed to increased screen time and changes in mental well-being. This study investigates the relationship between daily smartphone usage, measured in hours, spent on different platforms for social media, gaming, and productivity, and its potential effects on young adults aged 21-25 in the United States. Specifically, we aim to examine correlations between daily usage and variables including gender, number of users/daily time, and daily usage and emotional status. We chose to research this topic due to its relevance to young adults who often experience high levels of digital engagement, hoping to better comprehend the potential correlation between entertainment screen time, productivity and screen time’s effect on emotional well being within this subject group.

To conduct our research, we examined two separate data sets: Smartphone Usage and Behavioral Data gave us an understanding of smartphone usage patterns at the individual level, while Social Media Usage and Emotional Well-Being provided us a better grasp on user-level social media engagement and emotional state labeling. By exploring both behavioral and emotional indicators, we aimed to better understand how patterns of smartphone usage may influence productivity and mental health in this specific age group.

Our findings indicate that users showed significantly higher engagement with entertainment apps, such as gaming or social media apps. However, contrary to our initial hypothesis, leisure apps did not directly correlate with reduced usage of productivity apps. Instead, we observed that there is a stronger positive relationship between time spent on entertainment apps and measures of mental well-being. Users reported that they more frequently used apps that contributed to favorable feelings, suggesting that emotional reinforcement may be a significant factor behind app engagement and screen time. These results show the emotional motives behind smartphone usage among young adults, indicating that the role of screen time may act more as a form of emotional self-regulation than as a detractor from productivity. Understanding these patterns can help guide future approaches to mental health and technology use, emphasizing the importance of both quantity and emotional quality of digital engagement in shaping wellbeing.

Research Question¶

By looking at overall screen time data and its relation to daily function and emotional well-being, how does daily smartphone usage in hours spent on social media and gaming apps affect the total usage of productivity apps among young adults aged 21-25 in the US over the course of a day?

Background and Prior Work¶

Smartphones are a technology that almost every young adult has in their back pocket. These devices have become intertwined with the human experience as they are a valuable tool that allows for communication, direction, and entertainment. However, these devices have become a large source of distraction, contributing to increased screen time, diminishing attention spans, and less productivity.

This trend has become something that researchers have become increasingly aware of as smartphones have integrated into young adults' lives and have started to have effects on cognition. Although there is still little research on the long term effects of smartphone usage on cognitive effects, there have been many studies looking into the immediate effects of smartphone usage on attention span.

At Temple University, the Psychology Department conducted a study on the Links between smartphones and cognitive abilities called; “Smartphones and Cognition: A Review of Research Exploring the Links between Mobile Technology Habits and Cognitive Functioning” by Henry H Wilmer, Lauren E Sherman, & Jason M Chein, where they explored effects on attention, memory, delay of gratification, and everyday cognitive functions from smartphone usage ^. These researchers highlighted concerns that frequent smartphone usage could be associated with reduced working memory, less self-control relating to delay gratification, and an overall increased reliance on these devices for cognitive functions. The study strengthens this argument by citing research previously conducted on the topic and explaining how these studies were carried out.

One of the negative impacts on attention that is highlighted in this research paper is "Frequent media multitasking"1, a behavior that is inherently tied to the nature of smartphone use. The researchers refer to this action as endogenous interruptions, where a user's thoughts drift towards smartphone-related activities, where “The ‘mere presence’ of a cell phone may produce diminished attention and worsened task-performance, especially for tasks with high cognitive demands.” 2 The reasoning for this is likely due to a desire for more immediate gratification once an ongoing task is no longer rewarding. Thus, these "interruptions cause up to a 4x delay in completion of a primary task "3, emphasizing how smartphones can affect productivity, focus, and the mental state of users. Additionally, "In the presence of distractor stimuli during sustained attention task, frequent media multitaskers perform worse and exhibit more right prefrontal activity" 4 suggesting that habitual multitasking can often facilitated by smartphone usage can be a cause for less productivity, making everyday task seem much more challenging.

The review also draws attention to how smartphone use affects both focused and sustained attention, particularly through constant interruptions. These interruptions are classified as either endogenous, which are internally driven urges to check one’s phone, or exogenous, like notifications or vibrations, which can disrupt attention even when ignored. For example, Stothart et al. found that simply hearing a phone notification, without even interacting with the device, significantly reduced performance on an attention-based task. 5 Similarly, the presence of a smartphone in one’s visual field has been shown to impair performance on tasks requiring higher cognitive effort. 2 These findings suggest that smartphones not only distract us during use but can impair cognitive functioning even when passively present. Over time, repeated interruptions may reduce our baseline ability to maintain sustained attention, making everyday tasks feel more difficult and draining. While more long term studies are needed, the existing evidence indicated a strong link between habitual smartphone engagement and a diminished capacity to focus.

These major effects on cognition are especially relevant for young adults aged 21–25, a period marked by critical transitions into independence, careers, and personal development. While smartphones offer useful tools such as navigation, communication, and organization apps, they also introduce constant opportunities for distraction. Productivity tools now compete for attention against more stimulating social media and gaming platforms. Research shows that while smartphones are often used for beneficial tasks, their constant presence can undermine focus. For instance, one study found that although a majority of users report using their smartphones for practical tasks, many also described the device as more of a distraction than a help, with noticeable shifts in behavior linked to compulsive checking habits7. Joshi et al. (2022) observed that young adults frequently engage in smartphone multitasking, a behavior tied to broader trends in digital distraction and reduced sustained attention8. Beyond attention, emotional well-being is also at stake. Exposure to negative or overwhelming content—such as during "doomscrolling," the compulsive habit of endlessly consuming distressing news or social media posts—has been linked to increases in anxiety and depressive symptoms. For many, the smartphone is no longer just a communication tool but has evolved into a constant companion, filling idle time and delivering frequent dopamine hits, often at the expense of mental health and productivity.

Hypothesis¶

We hypothesize that young adults aged 21-25 who spend more than 4 hours per day in total on social media and gaming apps will exhibit significantly lower usage of productivity applications, along with higher average daily screen time, compared to those who limit entertainment-focused app usage to under 2 hours per day. We further expect that higher usage patterns will be associated with more frequent emotional states, particularly in categories such as anxiety, depression, and anger, as captured by the dominant emotion labels in our second dataset.

This hypothesis is grounded in the assumption that excessive engagement with entertainment apps may displace time that could be spent on cognitively beneficial or goal-oriented activities, such as using calendar tools, note-taking apps, or other productivity platforms. In turn, this substitution may contribute to reduced emotional well-being by fostering feelings of inefficiency, overstimulation, or social comparison.

Dataset #1: Smartphone usage and Behavior Dataset This dataset captures smartphone usage patterns at the individual level, making it highly relevant for modeling behavioral tendencies and digital well-being. Key variables include demographic descriptors (Age, Gender, and Location), quantitative metrics: (Daily_Screen_Time_Hours, Total_App_Usage_Hours, and Number_of_Apps_Used), and category specific engagement (Specific app categories: Social_Media_Usage_Hours, Productivity_App_Usage_Hours, Gaming_App_Usage_Hours). The data types are a mix of integers, floats, and categorical strings. These variables serve as proxies for attention allocation, time prioritization, and lifestyle routines. A basic inspection revealed no missing values or data-type mismatches, and summary statistics show logical ranges (e.g., screen time hours between 0 and 15). However, to adjust for our research question, we cleaned the data to reflect our focus on 21 - 25 year olds. Preprocessing steps include standardizing usage hours and constraining our age range to meet our research question. This data set is relevant to our research question as it demonstrates users' overall screen time in hours, additionally breaking down the amount of hours spent on specific apps like social media apps, gaming apps, and productivity apps. Thus allowing us to get an understanding of young adults' screen time distribution and to understand if social media/ gaming apps are really taking away time from productivity apps. When it came to missing values in this data set, we chose to get rid of incomplete data only if its incompleteness seemed purely random; otherwise, we used a strategy of complete-case analysis where if our group was faced with incomplete data, we would delete a row of data missing one of its entries. To avoid introducing a bias, we ensured that the missing data did not follow any systematic pattern. This data did not have missing values. The only changes made were to sort out the entries within our specified age group (21-25) and convert the original ‘Age’ column values from strings to integers.

Dataset #2: Social Media and Emotional Well-Being The dataset represents a complete profile of user level social media engagement and emotional state labeling, supporting our modeling goals. Key variables include platform specific behavior (Platform, Posts_Per_Day, Likes_Received_Per_Day, Comments_Received_Per_Day, Messages_Sent_Per_Day), and emotional responses (Dominant_Emotion). Most features are numerical, with some categorical fields like Gender, Platform, and Dominant_Emotion. These datasets act as proxies for emotional engagement, platform-specific intensity, and digital social interaction. A preliminary EDA across splits shows consistency, suggesting high quality. Inspecting the data, we adjusted the ages to reflect our focused group of only 21-25 year olds, and for consistency, we adjusted the data in the Daily usage time from minutes to hours, which is the metric we are using to measure screen time. Overall, this data set supports our research as it gives deeper insight into how these apps are affecting young adults' mental states and their emotional reactions to the amount of time spent on social/gaming apps. When it came to missing values in this data set, we chose to eliminate incomplete data only if its incompleteness seemed purely random; otherwise, we used a strategy of complete-case analysis where if our group was faced with incomplete data, we would delete a row of data is missing one of its entries. To avoid introducing a bias, we ensured that the missing data did not follow any systematic pattern. This data did not have missing data.

To create a comprehensive behavioral model, we will analyze both the smartphone usage data and the social media data separately, as each data set represents a distinct group of individuals. However, we plan to use these datasets to complement one another and to gain a deeper understanding and analysis when it comes to answering our overall research question. In doing so we will take into account the differences and similarities between the data and explore how the data can support each other. This approach would allow for the integration of general behavioral data with platform-specific social media engagement metrics, enabling richer models for predicting outcomes like dominant emotion or app category preference. Minor column renaming and normalization will be required to unify the merged dataset. When it comes to our overall question, using both data sets together helps us to understand how smartphone usage may disrupt daily productivity and increase engagement with social and gaming apps. Furthermore, the Social Media and Wellbeing dataset helps to reveal how social media apps also have an effect on our dominant emotions that can negatively impact one's mental well being. Together, these datasets provide a more holistic view of then relations between digital habits, productivity, and emotional health.

Ethics & Privacy¶

Screen time data can be highly personal, potentially revealing sensitive details about user behavior, app preferences, and daily routines. To protect user privacy, we will only use anonymized and publicly available datasets that have been ethically sourced and shared with consent. We will avoid using any data obtained from platforms or applications without explicit user permission or proper anonymization. Additionally, we will ensure that none of our analysis could indirectly re-identify individuals, especially if we cross reference multiple data sources.

Our focus is on individuals aged 21-25 in the United States, but we recognize that existing datasets may not fully represent this group across all socioeconomic, racial, geographic, or educational lines. Self-reported screen time data may also introduce recall bias, while datasets collected from certain platforms (eg. app-based trackers) may disproportionately reflect certain demographics (eg. tech savvy or higher income users).

To detect biases before during and after:

  • We will prioritize datasets that include objective measures of screen time (eg. device-tracked logs rather than self-reports)
  • We will stratify our analysis by demographics such as age, gender, income, education level, and geographic region where available
  • We will report any gaps or skews in the data and avoid overgeneralizing results beyond the represented population
  • We will compare multiple datasets if possible to check for consistency and reduce reliance on a single biased source.

There is a risk that our findings could be overinterpreted to stigmatize high smartphone use or productivity patterns among certain groups. For instance, lower productivity app usage may not necessarily indicate poor time management, instead it could reflect socioeconomic differences in job types, access to resources, or cultural habits. There is also the possibility that our analysis could unintentionally promote overgeneralized conclusions about smartphone use and attention without considering context.

To address these concerns:

  • We will clearly qualify our claims based on the limitations of our data
  • We will emphasize that correlation does not imply causation and note other potential factors influencing app usage behavior
  • We will contextualize our conclusions to prevent harmful or misleading narratives about specific user groups.

Discusison and Conclusion¶

This study suggests that while high engagement with social media and gaming apps may not directly reduce productivity app use, it does have a role in affecting the emotional states of its users. From the section Patterns in Productivity vs. Leisure App Usage, we did not find any inverse relationship nor any significant correlation between productivity and leisure app usage. Meanwhile, in Social Media Engagement and Emotional Well-Being, user behaviors like receiving more likes corresponded to ‘Happiness’ dominating. We also found in this section that different social media platforms had different dominant emotions (such as “Happiness” for Instagram or “Anger” for Twitter) which suggests that users of different platforms will experience different emotions based on the platform they choose to interact with.

This study builds on previous work by exploring smartphone app usage patterns and examining their potential effects on emotional well-being and productivity behaviors in young adults. Going back to earlier research from Wilmer, Sherman, and Chein at Temple University that emphasized the effects of smartphone usage, our findings turn to look towards how the usage is reflected in young adults. Our hypothesis predicted that users who spend more than 4 hours daily on social media and gaming apps would record lower usage of productivity apps and report more frequent negative emotions (anxiety, anger, or sadness). While our first simple data exploration showed that users do spend more time on social and gaming apps than on productivity tools, we ultimately did not find any strong relationship. This suggests that productivity app usage may not be strictly displaced by leisure activities, as was initially theorized, and could instead be influenced by other personal factors like personality preferences or life schedule.

For the second part of our hypothesis, we did find that social media engagement had implications on emotional well-being. This was illustrated by how the users who received more likes had ‘Happiness’ as their dominant emotion, while the other emotions (neutral, sadness, anxiety, and boredom) all correlated to lower like counts. This implies that engagement on social media may not solely rely on the amount of time spent on the platform, but from the quality of the activity. Additionally, no social media platform had an overall dominant emotion of ‘Neutral’, suggesting that spending time on platforms amplifies emotions.

There are some limitations to this research. Our two datasets represent two different sets of user populations that are not inherently related to each other. Additionally, emotional description can be more complex than the labels provided in the second dataset. Further research can be made addressing these limitations by using a broader dataset that collects additional information.
